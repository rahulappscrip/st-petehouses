Inherited & Probate Florida Pricing guide

How to price an inherited home in Florida.

A practical, Florida-specific framework for heirs — probate timing, the federal stepped-up basis, honest local comps, and choosing between a listing or a cash sale. From John Gardepe, a local St. Petersburg buyer who's helped families through this many times.

JG By John GardepeCo-Founder & Head of Acquisitions · We Buy St Pete Houses Published May 27, 2026 10 min read

If you've recently inherited a home in Florida and you're trying to figure out what it's actually worth, you're in the right place. Pricing an inherited home in Florida comes down to three things: understanding where the property stands in probate, knowing your stepped-up tax basis so you can estimate the real financial outcome, and using recent local comparable sales — adjusted for the home's actual condition — to set a realistic price range.

You also have a choice about how to sell: listing on the open market for potentially more money, or accepting a cash offer for speed and certainty. This guide walks through each piece of that framework in plain language.

This is general information, not legal or tax advice. Every estate is different. Work with a Florida probate attorney or tax professional for guidance specific to your situation. What this guide will give you is a clear, practical way to think through pricing so you can make informed decisions during what is often a difficult time.

What a Florida-specific pricing framework looks like.

A practical pricing framework for an inherited home in Florida starts with four questions: Where does the property stand in probate? What is the tax basis? What are comparable homes actually selling for nearby? And does speed or maximum price matter more to the heirs?

Definition

Florida-specific pricing framework for inherited homes: A structured approach that combines Florida probate status and expected timeline, the federal stepped-up tax basis, recent local comparable sales adjusted for condition, and the seller's priorities for speed versus top dollar — to arrive at a realistic price range rather than a single number.

The first step is clarifying probate status. Until the personal representative has legal authority to sell, the home can't close, and that timeline shapes everything from marketing strategy to how aggressively you price. Second, you need to pin down the property's tax basis. Under IRS Publication 551, inherited property generally receives a stepped-up basis equal to fair market value at the date of death, which means most heirs won't owe capital gains tax unless the home appreciates significantly after they inherit it.

Third, gather recent comparable sales in the same neighborhood and adjust for condition — an inherited home with deferred maintenance will not fetch the same price as a recently renovated one across the street. Fourth, decide whether your priority is maximizing gross price through a traditional listing or gaining speed and certainty through an as-is cash sale. Each path lands at a different point in the price range, and neither is automatically better.

One important detail that surprises many heirs: Florida does not impose a state estate tax or inheritance tax for decedents who died on or after January 1, 2005, and no Florida estate tax return is required (as of January 2025). Federal estate and income tax rules can still apply to larger estates, but the state itself will not be taking a cut. A fair price is a range built on probate timing, tax basis, honest condition assessment, and your own priorities — not a single number pulled from a guess or an emotional attachment. How probate timing affects your price and sale strategy.

Probate timing directly shapes pricing strategy because it determines how soon you can actually close a sale — and how much the estate will spend on carrying costs in the meantime. According to Florida Bar probate guidance (as of January 2024), a simple, uncontested estate involves a 3-month creditor-claims period after notice, and many straightforward probates remain open for roughly 5–6 months. More involved or contested estates can take considerably longer depending on the circumstances and court schedules.

Those months matter financially. While the estate is open, someone is paying property insurance, property taxes, HOA dues (if applicable), utilities, and basic maintenance. Even modest carrying costs — a few hundred dollars a month between insurance, lawn care, and utilities — add up steadily over a 5- or 6-month probate. Every month the home sits unsold, the net proceeds shrink.

This is where pricing and timeline become linked. If the heirs need to wrap up the estate quickly or want to minimize carrying costs, pricing the home closer to the lower end of the comparable range — or accepting a cash offer — can make sense. If the timeline is more flexible and the estate can absorb holding expenses, listing at a higher price and waiting for the right buyer may produce a better gross return. Neither approach is right or wrong; it depends on the estate's financial situation and the heirs' priorities.

It is also worth knowing that some steps can happen before probate is fully closed. In many cases, the personal representative can sign a purchase contract during probate, potentially contingent on court approval. However, the specifics depend on the case, and a Florida probate attorney should guide any binding decisions.

Every month an inherited home sits in probate without a pricing plan is a month of carrying costs quietly eating into the estate's bottom line.

Key Florida-specific factors that affect inherited home value.

Florida is not a one-size-fits-all market, and several state-specific factors can move the needle on what an inherited home is actually worth.

Location: coastal vs. inland. A waterfront or near-waterfront property in a place like St. Petersburg or Clearwater carries a different risk-and-reward profile than an inland home in a central Florida suburb. Flood zone designations, hurricane exposure, and proximity to the coast all affect both buyer demand and insurance costs.

Insurance realities. Florida's property insurance market is a major pricing factor. Homes in coastal or wind-exposed areas often carry significantly higher premiums, and older roofs or outdated electrical and plumbing systems can make a property difficult to insure at all. Buyers factor these costs into their offers. If the inherited home has a roof nearing the end of its useful life or older systems, expect that to weigh on price.

Age, condition, and deferred maintenance. Many inherited homes have sat with the same owner for decades. In the Tampa Bay area, that often means older concrete-block construction with original windows, dated kitchens, and deferred maintenance. Buyers — especially those relying on financing — will discount for condition, and appraisers will too.

Code compliance and permitting. Unpermitted additions, converted garages, and older electrical panels (like Federal Pacific or Zinsco) are common in Florida homes built before the 1990s. These issues can stall a financed sale or reduce the buyer pool to cash buyers willing to take on the risk.

HOA and community rules. If the inherited property is in a community with an HOA, factor in dues, special assessments, and any rental or sale restrictions that could limit your buyer pool.

Definition

Probate-adjusted valuation: An estimate of an inherited property's realistic market value that accounts for Florida-specific risks — including storm exposure, insurance costs, HOA obligations, and code-compliance issues — layered on top of standard comparable-sales analysis and probate timeline constraints.

If the inherited property was also a rental, many of these same maintenance and tenant issues apply. You can read more about the challenges of selling a rental property for additional context.

How to price an inherited home in Florida: a 5-step checklist.

Here is a practical, step-by-step approach tailored to Florida heirs:

  1. Confirm title and probate status.

    Work with the personal representative and a Florida probate attorney to verify probate is open, authority to sell has been (or will be) granted, and title is clear or on track. Nothing else matters until this step is handled.

  2. Identify your tax basis.

    Under IRS Publication 551 (as of January 2025), inherited property generally has a basis equal to fair market value at the date of death. If the executor files Form 706, an alternate valuation date (six months after death) may be elected. This stepped-up basis is what determines your potential capital gains. Talk to a tax professional to confirm your specific situation.

  3. Gather recent comparable sales.

    Pull sales of similar homes in the same neighborhood from the past three to six months. Adjust for differences in size, condition, and upgrades. An inherited home with original finishes and a 20-year-old roof will not match comps that were recently renovated.

  4. Estimate closing, probate, and carrying costs.

    Add up what the estate will spend between now and closing: insurance, property taxes, HOA dues, utilities, lawn maintenance, and any probate-related fees. These costs reduce your net proceeds and should inform how aggressively you price.

  5. Set a pricing range and choose your sale path.

    Based on your comps, condition, and carrying-cost timeline, set a realistic range. Then decide: list traditionally for a potentially higher gross price, sell as-is to reduce hassle and repair costs, or pursue a cash offer for speed and certainty. If a fast, as-is sale appeals to you, that option can align closing with your probate milestones.

Probate status + tax basis + honest comps + carrying costs = a realistic price range. That's the entire formula, and every step matters.

Common pricing mistakes heirs make.

Pricing an inherited home is rarely straightforward, especially when you're managing grief, family dynamics, and an unfamiliar process at the same time. Here are the mistakes I see most often — and how to sidestep them.

Overpricing based on emotional attachment.How to avoid it

Anchor your expectations in recent comparable sales within the same neighborhood, not in what the home meant to your family. Buyers price on comps and condition, not sentiment.

Assuming the home can be sold immediately.How to avoid it

Confirm probate status with a Florida probate attorney before you do anything else. Many heirs don't realize probate must be opened and the personal representative must have authority before a sale can close.

Overlooking the stepped-up basis and tax consequences.How to avoid it

Review the IRS basis overview or talk to your tax professional before setting a price. Understanding stepped-up basis can save you from making fear-based pricing decisions — or from missing a real tax obligation on large gains.

Underestimating carrying costs during probate.How to avoid it

Insurance, property taxes, HOA dues, and maintenance don't pause while the estate is open. Calculate your monthly holding costs early and factor them into your pricing timeline.

Using outdated or non-local comps.How to avoid it

Use sales from the past three to six months in the same immediate area, and adjust for condition. A comparable sale from a year ago — or from a different neighborhood — can badly mislead your pricing.

Cash offers and fast sale options for inherited Florida homes.

A bona fide cash buyer can simplify an inherited home sale in several ways: fewer contingencies, no lender-required appraisal or repair demands, the ability to purchase the property as-is, and closing timelines that can align with probate milestones — such as scheduling closing shortly after the creditor-claims period ends.

If you're weighing a cash offer, here's what to look for:

  • Clear, written terms. The offer should spell out the price, timeline, and any conditions in writing before you commit.
  • Proof of funds. A legitimate cash buyer will provide verification that the funds are available — not just a verbal promise.
  • No surprise fees. Understand upfront who pays closing costs and whether any service fees will be deducted.
  • Realistic closing timeline. The buyer should understand that probate governs the timeline and be willing to work within it.

The trade-off between listing with an agent and accepting a cash offer is real. A traditional listing may produce a higher gross sale price, but it also comes with agent commissions, potential repair requests, longer time on market, and the risk of buyer financing falling through. A credible cash buyer offers speed, certainty, and reduced holding and repair costs — but typically at a lower sale price. Neither path is universally better; the right choice depends on whether the estate's priority is maximizing the headline number or closing efficiently with less friction.

Tax basics when you sell an inherited home in Florida.

The tax picture for selling an inherited home in Florida breaks into two layers: state and federal.

Florida state taxes. Florida does not impose a state estate tax or inheritance tax for decedents who died on or after January 1, 2005, and no Florida estate tax return is required (as of January 2025). This is a meaningful relief — many states do levy an inheritance or estate tax, but Florida is not one of them.

Definition

Stepped-up basis (inherited property): Under federal tax rules, inherited property generally receives a basis equal to its fair market value at the date of the decedent's death (IRS Publication 551, as of January 2025). This means heirs are typically only taxed on appreciation that occurs after they inherit — not on the gains the original owner accumulated over decades.

Federal capital gains. If you sell the inherited home for more than its stepped-up basis, the difference is subject to federal capital gains tax. Because the basis resets at date-of-death value, many heirs who sell relatively soon after inheriting owe little or no capital gains. However, if you hold the property and it appreciates, or if significant improvements changed the value picture, you could face a taxable gain. Confirm your specific situation with a CPA or tax professional.

Federal estate tax. For 2024, the federal estate tax basic exclusion is $13,610,000 per individual, with portability allowing a surviving spouse to use the deceased spouse's unused exclusion (as of January 2024). In practice, the vast majority of Florida estates will not owe federal estate tax. For estates that do exceed the threshold, the federal estate tax uses a progressive rate structure ranging from 18% to 40%, with the top rate of 40% applying to amounts over $1,000,000 (as of January 2024).

Non-resident, non-citizen decedents. If the decedent was not a U.S. citizen or resident, different rules apply. The Form 706-NA filing threshold for non-resident non-citizen decedents is $60,000 in U.S.-situated assets, and this threshold is not indexed for inflation (as of January 2025). If you're dealing with an international estate, specialized legal and tax counsel is essential.

This section is general information, not legal or tax advice. Estate and income tax rules can interact in ways that depend heavily on estate size, family structure, and timing. For personalized planning — especially when multiple heirs or larger estates are involved — consult a Florida probate attorney or CPA.

Using comps and probate data to estimate market value.

To estimate what an inherited Florida home is actually worth, you need comparable sales that reflect reality — not hope.

Start by identifying recent sales of similar properties: same type (single-family vs. condo), similar square footage, in the same neighborhood or immediate area, and sold within the past three to six months. Older sales may not reflect current conditions, especially in Florida markets where values can shift quickly after hurricane seasons or insurance market changes.

Next, adjust for condition. Inherited homes often come with deferred maintenance that recently sold comps may not share. Picture an older St. Petersburg bungalow with original terrazzo floors, a roof approaching the end of its lifespan, and a kitchen that hasn't been updated since the 1980s. Across the street, a similar-sized home sold three months ago after a full renovation — new roof, updated kitchen, impact windows. That renovated sale is a data point, but it is not your comp without significant downward adjustment for condition. Think about what a buyer would need to spend to bring the inherited home up to that standard, and discount accordingly.

Also factor in the probate timeline. If the estate needs a faster close, you may want to price toward the lower end of the comparable range to attract offers quickly and reduce carrying costs. If the timeline is flexible, you have room to price higher and wait.

Here is a simple way to frame it: pricing an inherited home in Florida using comps means finding what similar homes sold for recently, subtracting for condition and needed repairs, and then adjusting your asking range based on how much time and carrying cost the estate can absorb. Your stepped-up tax basis acts as a financial guardrail — if the net sale price is close to or below that basis, capital gains are minimal, and you can focus purely on getting a fair market return.

Turning national checklists into a Florida-ready plan.

If you've been searching online for help pricing an inherited home, you've probably found plenty of national guides offering generic steps — confirm ownership, get an appraisal, estimate repairs, and so on. That advice is often solid as a starting point, but it rarely accounts for the details that actually matter in Florida.

Most national checklists don't mention that Florida has no state inheritance or estate tax for decedents dying on or after January 1, 2005 (as of January 2025) — so any tax guidance that assumes your state takes a cut may not apply to you. They also tend to skip Florida's probate timeline, including the 3-month creditor-claims period and the roughly 5–6 month window for simple estates noted in Florida Bar guidance (as of January 2024). And they almost never address the insurance and HOA realities that heavily influence buyer behavior in this state.

Here's how to adapt any online pricing checklist to Florida:

  • Check Florida probate rules first. Confirm whether probate has been opened and what the expected timeline looks like before you make any pricing or listing decisions.
  • Verify that tax information applies to Florida. If a national guide references a state inheritance tax, that does not apply here. Focus on the federal stepped-up basis rules and federal estate tax thresholds instead.
  • Factor in local insurance and HOA costs. These are significant in Florida and directly affect what buyers will pay. A national guide won't flag them.
  • Cross-check example prices against recent local sales. National averages are meaningless for pricing a specific home in a specific Florida neighborhood. Use comps from the past three to six months in your area.

Generic advice can give you a framework, but only Florida-specific research — grounded in real local data — gives you a price range you can trust. For more Florida-focused seller resources, it's worth going beyond the first page of national search results.

Frequently asked questions.

Do you have to pay taxes on inherited property that you sell in Florida?

Florida does not have its own estate or inheritance tax for decedents who died on or after January 1, 2005 (as of January 2025). However, if you sell the inherited home for more than its stepped-up basis — generally the fair market value at the date of death — you may owe federal capital gains tax on the difference. Very large estates could also face federal estate tax. Because the rules depend on estate size, holding period, and individual circumstances, it's worth consulting a tax professional to understand your specific exposure.

What is the typical probate timeline in Florida?

For a simple, uncontested estate, Florida Bar guidance indicates a 3-month creditor-claims period after notice, with many straightforward probates remaining open for roughly 5–6 months (as of January 2024). Contested estates or those with unusual assets or debts can take considerably longer. These timelines are general guidance, not guarantees — confirm your specific case with a Florida probate attorney.

What is a practical Florida-specific pricing framework for inherited homes?

A Florida-specific pricing framework blends four elements: (1) the property's probate status and expected timeline, (2) the federal stepped-up tax basis and any potential tax impact, (3) recent local comparable sales adjusted for actual property condition, and (4) the heirs' priorities — whether maximizing sale price or closing quickly matters more. Together, these produce a realistic price range rather than a single number.

Can I sell an inherited home in Florida before probate ends?

In many cases, the personal representative can enter into a purchase contract during probate, but the actual transfer of title and closing may require court approval or the completion of certain probate steps. The specifics vary by estate and county, so work closely with a Florida probate attorney before signing anything binding. Having a buyer lined up and a signed contract ready can speed things up once the court gives the green light.

  • Underestimating insurance, HOA, and maintenance carrying costs during probate
  • Relying on outdated or non-local comparable sales that don't reflect current market conditions
  • Skipping an honest assessment of the home's physical condition and needed repairs
